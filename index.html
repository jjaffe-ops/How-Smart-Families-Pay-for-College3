The Parent Move
Pick a number
Before the letters arrive — not after

Picture this. The acceptance letter arrives. Your student gets in. The moment you've both been working toward is right in front of you. You hug. Maybe you cry. You take a photo.

A few weeks later, the financial aid package comes. You open it. You read it twice. You do the math. The number is higher than you expected. Significantly higher. But your teen has already told their friends, looked up the dining halls on Instagram, and started imagining their life there. And they have no idea.

That's the moment most families' college financial planning breaks down. Not because the news is bad. Because the conversation that should have happened months ago never did.

The families who handle this well have one thing the others don't: they already had a number before the letter arrived. A ceiling. Something they'd agreed on together calmly, before any school made an offer that made saying no feel impossible.

This lesson is about building one number before that moment arrives — your family's total college budget. It's made up of what you can contribute and what your student can responsibly borrow. Every school on your list either fits inside it or it doesn't.

What you're actually looking at

The average private college lists at around $63,000 a year. Families actually pay closer to $36,000. But that gap varies wildly depending on your income, your assets, your state, and the specific schools on your list.

The number every family needs before applications go in
BB
Brad Baldridge
Certified financial planner, college funding specialist

"Financial constraints need to come up early and often," Baldridge told us. "A lot of parents don't put guardrails on it because they don't know what they're getting into. They say, you can go wherever you want — and then they realize some colleges have price tags that are out of reach."

His rule of thumb is the clearest framework we've found for families trying to set a student borrowing limit before the offers roll in:

Baldridge's Rule of Thumb — The First-Year Salary Rule
Total borrowing should not exceed your student's expected first-year income.

"A nurse coming out at $75,000 — that level of debt is manageable for most people. A social worker starting at $40,000? The math changes fast." If your student's career direction is still undecided, Baldridge's advice is to borrow on the lower end and leave room to adjust. "You can always revisit. But you can't undo the debt."

Walsh approaches the same question from a different angle: monthly cash flow. His guideline — keep loan payments to around 15 to 20 percent of your student's starting salary — is a useful gut-check once you have a number.

Parents and students aren't always on the hook for the same loans
BW
Brian Walsh
Head of Financial Planning, SoFi

A Parent PLUS loan stays in the parent's name. Permanently. "People don't fully appreciate who's responsible for paying the loan back," Walsh says. "A lot of families just assume that once the kid gets done with school, it's going to transfer to their name. That's not technically how it works."

If your student can't make payments after graduation, the PLUS loan is still yours — not theirs. That's an important structural difference between a Parent PLUS loan and a co-signed private loan. With a PLUS loan, the parent is the borrower. With a co-signed private loan, the student is the primary borrower and the parent is secondarily liable. Neither is inherently right or wrong. But they carry different implications for a parent's retirement, their credit, and their long-term financial picture.

The rules on Parent PLUS loans just changed

For years, Parent PLUS loans had no hard cap. Parents could borrow up to the full cost of attendance. Starting July 1, 2026, that changes. There are now annual and lifetime limits on what parents can borrow.

Action required

If your family was planning to use PLUS loans to cover a significant portion of a school's cost, go back to the net price calculator for any school on your list where that was the plan. Run the numbers again with the new caps applied. If the gap between what federal loans now cover and what the school costs is larger than your family can fill another way, that school needs a second look.

College money doesn't come from nowhere

College isn't a separate financial category. It competes for the same dollars as everything else. Every $10,000 borrowed for college is $10,000 not invested for retirement. Over time, that tradeoff compounds.

Every family draws that line somewhere. Drawing it before the offers arrive is the whole point of this lesson.

Build your family's borrowing ceiling
1
Write down one number.

What's the most your family could realistically contribute to college per year? A rough estimate is fine. Getting it out of your head and onto paper is the whole point of this step.

2
See what that number actually means.

Before you go further, pull up a loan calculator with your student. A $60,000 loan sounds manageable. A $700 monthly payment on a $40,000 salary feels very different. Make the debt real for your student — not just in the parent's head.

3
Apply Baldridge's First-Year Salary Rule.

Total student borrowing should not exceed your student's expected first-year salary. That's not a soft guideline — it's the number Baldridge uses as a ceiling with his own clients. If the career path is undecided, use a conservative estimate and plan to revisit.

4
Add both numbers. That's your family's ceiling.

If you can contribute $15,000 a year and your student's expected starting salary is $50,000, your ceiling is $110,000 total across four years. Any school whose net price comes in above that number needs either more aid or a harder conversation.
